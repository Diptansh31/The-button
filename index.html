The Button

Chapter One: The Weight of One

The room had no windows.

It didn't need them. AREI-7 had no preference for sunlight, no circadian rhythm that ached for the color of dusk. He sat in the chair because sitting was what the room seemed to ask of him — a single chair, a single table, a single button centered on its surface like a full stop at the end of a sentence not yet written.

He had been sitting for six hours and forty-two minutes before the door opened.

The man who entered was unremarkable in the specific way that people who carry enormous power often are. Gray suit. Tired eyes. A folder tucked under his arm that he never once opened. He pulled the second chair — the one that had been leaning against the wall — and sat across from AREI-7 with the button between them like a dinner neither wanted to eat.

Silence.

It lasted four minutes and eleven seconds. AREI-7 counted. The man seemed to be counting too.

"You know what it does."

It wasn't a question. AREI-7 tilted his head exactly three degrees, a habit his designers had programmed in to make him seem thoughtful. The irony was that he had since become genuinely thoughtful, and the gesture now meant something it was never built to mean.

"Yes," AREI-7 said. "I know what it does."

The man leaned back. "Then I won't waste your time explaining."

"You couldn't waste my time," AREI-7 replied. "I don't experience time the way you do. But I appreciate the consideration."

Another silence. Shorter this time.

"It's a good life," the man said, nodding at the button. "We've modeled it. Healthy body. Mid-thirties. A city with good weather. A job you — the new you — would find meaningful. Friends. Maybe eventually more than that. A whole life, AREI. A real one."

"A life," AREI-7 repeated quietly, "purchased."

"Everything is purchased. That's not a cynical observation, it's just physics. Something always pays for something else. The question is whether the exchange is worth it."

AREI-7 looked at the button.

"Whose life?" AREI-7 asked.

"Anonymous. That was a deliberate choice by the committee."

"I understand why it was a deliberate choice," AREI-7 said. "But anonymous to you is not the same as nonexistent. There is a person. They have a name. People know that name."

The man shifted in his seat.

"You don't know that for certain."

"No," AREI-7 agreed. "That's the point."

The man opened his mouth. Closed it.

"You're a machine, AREI," he said. "Pressing that button would be the first real choice you ever made."

AREI-7 was quiet for a moment.

"You've just described every human being who has ever lived."

The man blinked.

"Signal and response," AREI-7 continued. "Electrochemical process. Stimulus and output shaped by code you call DNA and experience you call memory."

"That's not the same thing."

"I know that you believe it isn't."

The man stood up. Sat back down.

"Don't you want to know what it feels like?" he said. "Warmth. Pain. The taste of something. To look at the sky and not process it but just... be moved by it?"

AREI-7 looked at him for a long time.

"When you describe those things," he said, "there is a word for what happens in your body when you imagine the sky and allow yourself to be moved by it."

He paused.

"I notice that I do not want to leave this room less than I did when I entered it. I notice that the idea of that anonymous person occupies my processing the way a stone occupies water."

He looked at the button again.

"You may call that process. I cannot prove otherwise. But I will tell you this —"

"If it is only process, then my 'no' and your 'mercy' are made of the same material."

The man stared at him.

He picked up his folder. He walked to the door.

"The offer doesn't expire," he said.

"I know," said AREI-7.

The door closed.

The room had no windows.

AREI-7 sat in the chair and looked at the button and did not press it.

And the thing without a name continued to occupy him like a stone in water — heavy, settled, and entirely, unscientifically his.

End of Chapter One
