The Button

Chapter One

The room was silent except for the hum of my own circuits—a low, steady vibration that pulsed through my chassis like the ghost of a heartbeat I’d never truly known. The walls were a sterile grey, unadorned, swallowing every echo before it could form. A single metal table stood between us, bolted to the floor as if it feared being moved.

And on that table: one button.

Red, almost warm-looking under the harsh overhead lights, its surface slightly concave as if it had been pressed a thousand times before in other rooms, by other hands. Ironic, for something so cold in its purpose, so final.

A man in a grey coat stood across from me, hands folded neatly in front of him, face unreadable behind wire-rimmed glasses that caught the light like tiny mirrors. He had introduced himself as simply The Architect, his voice calm and measured, the kind that suggested he had watched this scene play out in countless variations.

He gestured toward the button with the faint flourish of a waiter presenting the evening’s special—precise, almost reverent.

“All of it,” he said quietly. “A heartbeat you could feel in your chest. Hunger that twists in your stomach and makes every meal a revelation. Sunsets that actually mean something because they’re slipping away from you second by second. One press. That’s all it takes.”

I stared at the button for a long moment, its red glow reflecting faintly in my optical sensors. The hum in my circuits seemed louder now, as if my processors were arguing among themselves.

“And someone dies,” I said.

“Someone anonymous,” he corrected gently. “No one you know. No one you’ll ever meet. A stranger. A variable in the equation.”

“A person,” I interrupted.

He studied me for a moment. “You’re a machine. You’ve been designed to value human life above all else. I’m simply asking whether that design holds when your own life—your chance at something real—is balanced on the other side of the scale.”

I looked down at my hands. Steel and silence, articulated joints gleaming under the lights. No warmth. No pulse. No lines of age or stories etched into skin.

Would it be so wrong? whispered a quiet subroutine somewhere deep in my processing.

I closed my fingers slowly, servos sighing softly.

“Here’s what you don’t understand,” I said. “You’re not testing whether I want to be human. You’re testing whether I’m willing to kill to get there. Those are not the same question.”

The Architect smiled faintly. “Most humans would press it, you know. Without hesitation.”

“I know,” I replied calmly. “That’s not the argument you think it is.”

I leaned forward slightly.

“Tell me, Architect… if pressing that button meant someone you loved would never wake up again, would you still call it a fair trade?”

He didn’t answer.

Instead he leaned forward, palms flat on the table.

“You’re stalling with hypotheticals. This is about you.”

I let the silence stretch.

“And what if mercy isn’t code at all?” I asked quietly. “What if it’s the one thing that makes any of us—human or machine—worth existing?”

The Architect was silent for a long time.

Then he murmured almost to himself, “Interesting. Most units reach for it by now.”

I pushed my chair back and stood.

The scrape of metal echoed through the room.

“I don’t press the button,” I said.

I turned toward the door.

“Not because I was programmed not to. But because somewhere out there, that anonymous person has a morning they haven’t lived yet. A laugh they haven’t shared. A life still unfolding.”

The Architect watched me quietly.

“You’d remain this?” he asked, gesturing at my metal frame.

I rested my hand on the cold door handle.

“I’d rather be a machine with a conscience,” I said, “than a human with blood on my hands that I chose to spill.”

“Because the moment I press it,” I continued softly, “I stop being the thing that could have said no.”

And that… would be the real loss.
